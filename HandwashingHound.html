Handwashing Hound: Computer Vision System for Monitoring Effective Hand Hygiene

Research Paper (2015)
Google Science Fair Submission
KQED Article

Awards
  • Young Mind Awards Finalist in Research and Development Category
  • The Synopsys Outreach Foundation n+1 Prize Finalist
  • 2nd Place, Engineering Category, Synopsys Science Fair

Handwashing Hound is a computer vision system for ensuring compliance with the handwashing protocol specified by the Center for Disease Control (CDC). Our system uses a depth camera to observe a user as they wash their hands. The user's handwashing technique is evaluated by a combination of four software-based detectors: a water detector, a hand detector, a detector for hands in the zone below the faucet, and a soapy hand detector. Our system continuously monitors the state of the user’s hands with these detectors. By observing state transitions, our system correctly classifies whether the user is wetting their hands, scrubbing hands with soap, or rinsing their hands. The computer vision algorithm for detecting soapy hands is able to robustly detect the presence of soap bubbles on the hands of multiple users, despite variation in skin color and lighting conditions. The accuracy of our detectors was measured across four users using two different types of soap. The first three detectors were all more than 90% accurate while the soap detector was 82% accurate, on a per frame basis with over 3000 frames. The accuracy results are adequate for the intended purpose, as the system is able to process several frames per second in real time.

Handwashing Hound is a successful prototype and is able to recognize all the stages of the CDC handwashing protocol through the four key detectors. Our system can be used as a compliance tool in the healthcare and food industries, or as an educational tool.
